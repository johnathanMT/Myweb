Cosmology

Unraveling the mysteries of the universe — from the Big Bang to dark matter, black holes to the cosmic web.

The Scale of Everything

The universe spans approximately 93 billion light-years in diameter — and that's just the observable portion. What lies beyond our cosmic horizon remains one of the greatest open questions in science.

The Milky Way contains roughly 200–400 billion stars. The observable universe contains roughly 2 trillion galaxies. If every grain of sand on Earth represented a star in our galaxy, there would still be more galaxies than grains of sand on every beach combined.

Key Topics I Explore

Big Bang Theory

The universe began ~13.8 billion years ago from an infinitely dense singularity and has been expanding ever since.

Black Holes

Regions of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing — not even light — can escape the event horizon.

Dark Matter

~27% of the universe is made of dark matter — invisible to light but detectable through gravitational effects.

Dark Energy

The mysterious force (~68% of the universe) driving the accelerating expansion of spacetime itself.

Stellar Evolution

How stars are born in nebulae, live through nuclear fusion, and die as white dwarfs, neutron stars, or black holes.

Gravitational Waves

Ripples in spacetime first detected in 2015, confirming Einstein's century-old prediction from General Relativity.

A Timeline of the Universe

t = 0 — The Big Bang

All energy, matter, and space compressed into a singularity; expansion begins.

t = 380,000 yrs — First Light

Protons capture electrons forming hydrogen; the universe becomes transparent (Cosmic Microwave Background).

t = 200 million yrs — First Stars

Population III stars ignite, seeding the cosmos with heavier elements through nuclear fusion.

t = 9.2 billion yrs — Our Sun Born

The Solar System coalesces from a spinning cloud of gas and dust.

t = 13.8 billion yrs — Now

You are here — a conscious arrangement of stardust, contemplating its own origin.
